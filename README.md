## What Actually Makes StarVPN Different

Most VPN providers run their servers in datacenters. Those IPs are registered to hosting companies, and websites — especially streaming platforms and anti-bot systems — have been flagging them for years. That's why you hit captchas on Google, why Netflix throws the proxy error, why survey platforms block you.

StarVPN's residential IPs are different. They're sourced from real home internet connections through actual ISPs. When you connect through StarVPN, you appear as a regular home user in whatever location you've selected. Websites see a Comcast IP in Texas or a BT IP in London — not a DigitalOcean server in Virginia.

The service runs on a global infrastructure backed by Cloudflare's load balancing network, with over 2,000 access points worldwide. It supports WireGuard, OpenVPN, and AmneziaWG — the last one being an obfuscated WireGuard fork that's particularly useful for punching through aggressive firewalls in places like China or Iran.

All plans also include both VPN access and SOCKS proxy access simultaneously. That's a fairly unique feature — most providers make you pick one or the other.

## What Protocols Does It Support?

StarVPN supports three protocols:
- **WireGuard** — fast and modern, one device per server, up to 5 devices total across servers
- **OpenVPN** — widely compatible, up to 5 simultaneous devices
- **AmneziaWG** — obfuscated WireGuard variant, designed to bypass deep packet inspection and firewalls

The AmneziaWG support is worth mentioning because it makes StarVPN one of the few residential VPNs that can also function in high-censorship environments. It disguises VPN traffic as standard HTTPS, which is a meaningful feature if you're operating in regions with aggressive network filtering.
